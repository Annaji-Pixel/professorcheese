Module 7: Pulleys (When Not To Use Them)

Pulleys are an alternative to using gears. They are great for some things, but in FLL, they are not used very often. In this module, we'll explore what they are, when to use them (rare), when not to, and why.

What are Pulleys?

Pulleys are wheel-and-belt systems used to transmit rotational motion from one shaft to another. Terms such as driver pulley and follower pulley can still apply here.

Below is an example of a pulley. However, Studio has limits. In reality, both the wheels you see below must have an elastic around them. It's the part that allows the pulleys to transmit rotational motion with each other.

Studio can't represent parts that can easily be modified, like wires connected to motors or elastics that support pulleys. It's encouraged that you physically build the model above and attach an elastic between the two wheels to understand how pulleys work. Torque and speed can be modified by pulleys as well.

Do We Use Them in FLL?

Uncommon. Pulleys are rarely used by any teams in FLL because of certain limitations when it comes to them. Gears are almost always better than pulleys in FLL. FLL competitions reward rigid systems. Gears are rigid. That doesn't mean pulleys are useless.

  • Elastics/belts can tear, lose their flexibility, and wear out over time. This is not a huge concern when it comes to gears.
  • They don't provide consistent results. Unlike gears which lock with each other, pulleys have elastics that can simply slip over their wheels, which poses uncertainty. If you want torque, gears provide more of it, and with much more certainty.
  • Their special features (that make them distinct from gears) are rarely needed.

2 Important Pulley Features

Pulleys have 2 important features that gears don't give.

  • Pulleys are parts-efficient.
  • 2 wheels in a pulley can move in the same direction, gears don't have this.
